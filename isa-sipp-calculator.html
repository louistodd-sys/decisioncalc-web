Should you overpay your mortgage in 2026?

With mortgage rates still elevated compared to the historic lows of 2020–2022, overpaying your mortgage is one of the most powerful financial moves available to UK homeowners. Every pound you overpay reduces your outstanding balance, which means you pay less interest from that point forward — and the savings compound over the remaining term.

How much can you save by overpaying?

On a £200,000 mortgage at 4.5% with 20 years remaining, overpaying just £200 per month saves approximately £22,000 in interest and cuts nearly 4 years from the term. The savings are front-loaded — overpaying early in your mortgage term has a dramatically larger impact than overpaying later, because interest is calculated on the remaining balance.

What is the 10% overpayment rule?

Most UK mortgage lenders allow you to overpay up to 10% of your outstanding balance each year without incurring early repayment charges (ERCs). For example, on a £200,000 mortgage you could overpay up to £20,000 per year — or around £1,667 per month. Always check your mortgage terms before overpaying, as ERCs can be significant (typically 1–5% of the amount overpaid above the limit).

Overpaying vs investing — which is better?

Overpaying your mortgage gives you a guaranteed, tax-free return equal to your mortgage interest rate. Investing in a Stocks and Shares ISA has historically returned 6–8% annually over long periods, but with volatility and no guarantee. If your mortgage rate is above 4.5%, overpaying is often the lower-risk choice. If your rate is below 3%, investing the difference may generate better long-term returns — especially for higher rate taxpayers using a SIPP.

Can I overpay a fixed rate mortgage?

Yes — most fixed rate mortgages in the UK allow overpayments of up to 10% of the outstanding balance per year without penalty. Some lenders are more flexible than this. If you want to overpay more than 10%, you may need to wait until your fixed rate period ends and you switch to a new deal, or accept the early repayment charge.
